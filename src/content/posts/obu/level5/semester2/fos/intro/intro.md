## Introduction

- This module introduces core principles and practices that underpin secure computing and information systems. The module develops an understanding of security goals and common threat models, then builds the mathematical and cryptographic foundations needed to reason about confidentiality, integrity, authenticity and trusted services. 
- Students will examine classical and modern cryptography, including symmetric and asymmetric techniques, hashing, message authentication and digital signatures, and consider how these mechanisms support secure protocols and architectures.
- Through guided activities and independent study, students will learn to evaluate the security of existing systems, communicate findings to technical and non-technical audiences, and work with legal, professional, ethical and social responsibilities in mind.

## Module Aims

The aim of this module is to study of information, computer and network security and ways that computer systems can be secured. This module will look at the technological, ethical and professional issues involved in securing and assessing the security level of a modern networked computer system.

## Teaching Contents

- Foundational concepts of security
- Mathematical Foundation of Cryptography – integer arithmetic, divisibility, GCD, Euclidean Algorithm, Modular Arithmetic
- Mathematical Foundation of Cryptography - Prime numbers, factorization, quadratic congruence and their applications in cryptography
- Classical Cryptography - Substitution Ciphers, Stream, Block Ciphers, DES, and AES 
- Symmetric & Asymmetric Cryptography – RSA, ElGamal, etc.
- Integration, Authenticity, Confidentiality -Hash, MDC, MAC, digital signature.

- Ethical Hacking tools and technique
- How to find the vulnerabilities of a website or web app
- Threats and Attacks, common issues and exploits
- Secure Architecture
- Social Engineering, Ethics, professional and legal issues
